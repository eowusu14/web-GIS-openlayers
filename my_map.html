National Railway Network, Canada

Layer Information

The National Railway Network (NRWN) is a geometric and attributive description of the Canadian rail network. The dataset was published by the Natural Resources Canada (Geogratis)
The NRWN layers consist of the features classes: Railway Track Segment, Railway Station, Railway Crossing and Railway Subdivision Name. Descriptive attributes include amongst others: Subdivision Name, Junction Type, Crossing Type, Level of Crossing, Station Name, Station Type, Structure Type.
The detailed description about the different layers are presented below:

Railway Track

Railway tracks in Canada are an important transportation infrastructure that connects cities and towns across the country. They are used to transport goods and materials, as well as passengers, and are a vital part of the country's economy. There are many different railway companies operating in Canada, including Canadian National Railway and Canadian Pacific Railway. These companies operate both passenger and freight trains, and their tracks can be found in many different parts of the country. Railway Tracks are Infrastructure including two steel rails which provides a guide for the movement of trains and other equipment. Railway Crossing

Railway crossings in Canada are places where a road crosses over or under a railway track. They are an important part of the country's transportation network, allowing vehicles to travel between different areas while also ensuring the safe passage of trains. There are many different types of railway crossings in Canada, including level crossings (where the road and track are at the same elevation), grade separations (where the road and track are on different levels), and pedestrian crossings (where pedestrians and sometimes bicycles can cross the tracks). On this web map page, railway crossings in Canada are shown as yellow with black 'X' points on the map. Railway Subdivision Name

Railway subdivisions in Canada are sections of railway track that are identified by a specific name or number. These subdivisions are used to organize and manage the railway system, and they often correspond to specific geographic areas or corridors. For example, a railway subdivision might be named after a nearby city or town, or it might be given a number that corresponds to a particular route or line.
